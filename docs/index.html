My profile

Welcome to my academic webpage!

I am a postdoctoral researcher at the department of Computational Cognitive Science at Tilburg University. My research focuses on motor learning and interpersonal coordination, particularly in the context of joint action. I study how people learn to coordinate their movements when they collaborate to achieve shared goals. Think about team sports, dancing in pairs, assistive health care, or shared household activities.

I find my inspiration in ecological psychology and in the complex and dynamical systems theory. This means that I see human movement as an emergent and embodied phenomenon that should be studied in its natural environment. Participants in my experiments should be able to explore, discover, learn and develop – like they do in the real world. Only then may I, as a researcher, find out why they move the way they move.

Career path

My background is in human movement sciences. In 2021, I graduated from the University of Groningen with a master’s thesis on the control of upper-limb movements during precision aiming, supervised by Prof. Raoul Bongers. Thereafter, I moved to France to write a doctoral dissertation on interpersonal movement coordination under the supervision of Profs. Reinoud Bootsma and Rémy Casanova. At the University of Aix-Marseille, I pioneered the “ball-and-beam task” – a novel experimental paradigm for joint action. In April 2025, I defended my dissertation and obtained my PhD degree.

Upon my return to the Netherlands, I continued my career with a series of postdoctoral research projects. These projects took me from the Donder’s institute in Nijmegen, to the University Medical Center in Groningen and back to the Radboud University Medical Center in Nijmegen. Along the way, I broadened my horizon by studying not only upper body motor control, but also human speech production, and the development of health care robots for people with dementia.

In 2026, the European Commission (Marie Sklodowska-Curie Actions) awarded me with a two-year Postdoctoral Fellowship for MATCH (see below for more details). This fellowship brought me to the University of Tilburg, where I further develop the ball-on-board experimental paradigm under the supervision of Profs. Travis Wiltshire and Wim Pouw.

MATCH

MATCH ("Motor Adaptation Through Complexity Matching in Harmonized Joint Action") is a two-year experimental research program funded by the Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions of the European Union. The project investigates whether joint action training can accelerate the acquisition of new motor skills, with potential applications in rehabilitation practice, such as upper-limb recovery after stroke. Current rehabilitation programs often focus on individual practice and may underutilize the social and collaborative dimensions of movement. Drawing on insights from complexity science, the project examines the hypothesis that practicing motor skills together with others can improve motor skill acquisition. As Principal Investigator, I am developing a novel ball-on-board paradigm that can be used for experimenting in Mindlabs, Tilburg. In this role, I will supervise a team of talented research trainees who will conduct a series of experimental studies with the task.
